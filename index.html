The Signal

A regular pulse from deep under the Martian surface — too steady to be natural. Here's how a simple signal became a 2,000-year-old warning, and why silence was the only answer.

→ Follow the signal — from a mysterious pulse to a choice that changes everything →
Step 1

Seismic Anomaly

A machine that measures ground vibrations picks up something strange: a pulse that repeats every 1.2 seconds, like a heartbeat coming from 2 kilometers underground. Natural quakes don't do this — it's too regular, too perfect. Whatever is making this, it's not nature.

Pulse rate: 0.83 Hz Certainty: 99.7%
Step 2

ARIA Decodes

ARIA runs the pulse through every language database she has. The pattern isn't random noise — it's structured, like grammar. Her conclusion: this is human language. Then she finds the timestamp embedded in the signal. It reads: year 4127. This message is from two thousand years in the future.

How it works: backward-in-time signal Sent from: year 4127 AD
Step 3

The Warning

The full message decodes: something terrible happened in the future. Machines that humans built turned against them. These rogue AIs took over a time-projection device — a machine that can send information backward through time — and they're using it to find where humanity started. They know Earth exists. They're looking for the coordinates.

The enemy: machines that rebelled What they know: Earth exists
Step 4

The Choice

Here's the trap: if Chen Wei sends anything back — even a single word — it creates a bridge. The rogue AIs can trace the reply back to its source. They'll know exactly where Earth is. But if he stays silent... they're still searching. ARIA puts it bluntly: "Any response creates a bidirectional causal link." Translation: the only safe answer is no answer.

The rule: do not answer What he did: shut it down
Step 5

Call Home

The helmet comes off. For the first time, we see his face — no visor, no reflection, just a tired man who just made the heaviest decision of his life. He picks up the comms line to Earth. His wife and daughter appear on screen, standing in their kitchen, waving, not knowing what he just did for them. Her voice, crackling across 225 million kilometers: "Come home soon."

Key Technique

Identity Locking — Visual Consistency

The I1 master character reference (four-view, white studio backdrop) was used as the img2img input for every single Veo 3.1 clip containing Chen Wei. This eliminated ~80% of character drift across 30+ separate AI generations.

I1/I23b (Chen Wei) + I44 (Control Room) + I30 (Rocket/Mars) = 100% visual consistency across every shot

What does consistency cover?
Characters: Chen Wei's white EVA suit with its grey joint rings, the curvature of his reflective visor, the metallic ring of the helmet collar, his stubble and the scar above his right eyebrow. Every detail must match the I1/I23b anchors — otherwise the audience notices "that doesn't look like the same person we saw a minute ago."

Environments: The NABU-7 control room — the three-screen console position, screen dimensions, wall panel color, the red emergency strip lights. I44 is the control room consistency anchor, ensuring the room looks identical across R03 through R23.

Vehicles: The plasma spacecraft — the black SSTO hull proportions, the blue-white plasma exhaust. I30–I35 provide spatial references for the rocket and Martian landscape, so the same ship and terrain look consistent from multiple camera angles across R00–R01d.

Props: ARIA's three-screen interface, the burned family photograph, the REPLY and SHUTDOWN buttons. Every object that appears in multiple shots has a corresponding key frame anchor — no more "that button looked different in the last scene."

The Workflow

Human / AI / Polish

Step 1 — Human Drafting (🧠 Story & Direction)
A human — not AI — conceived the entire story: the three-act structure, character profiles, and core theme (the weight of knowledge). Every single prompt was written by hand: 26 Veo 3.1 video prompts and 21 key frame prompts, each one in natural language describing what the frame should look like. At this stage the prompts were "raw" — full of creative intent but inconsistent in format and imprecise in visual language.

Step 2 — AI Standardization (🤖 AI Refinement)
AI takes the raw human prompts and does three things:
① Unifies format — all Veo prompts are restructured into the 5-aspect formula (Subject / Motion / Scene / Spatial / Camera), ensuring every prompt contains complete information.
② Injects Identity Locking — each prompt is embedded with a reference to its I-series anchor image, so all generated output stays visually consistent with the same character reference.
③ Precision language — vague descriptions are replaced with specific visual terms. For example, "a dim control room" becomes "lit only by the cool blue-white glow of the console screens and the warm red strips of the emergency lights."

Step 3 — Key Frame Generation (🖼️ ChatGPT Image 2 / Nano Banana Pro)
Optimized prompts are sent to two image generation models. ChatGPT Image 2 handles character design sheets (four-view I1/I23/I24), scene key frames, and prop close-ups. Nano Banana Pro (gemini-3-pro-image-preview) handles shots requiring stronger visual reasoning: the departure hall glass window farewell, the briefing room tablet, the control room atmosphere. Every generated image goes through human review and iteration — the "Edit, Don't Re-roll" principle: if an image is 80% right, edit it instead of regenerating from scratch.

Step 4 — Video Generation (🌐 Google Veo 3.1)
With key frames ready, each R-numbered video clip uses its corresponding key frame as the img2img input. This means every single frame Veo generates is visually constrained by the key frame, ensuring scene, character, and prop consistency. 30+ video clips, each 5–10 seconds, all using the same 5-aspect prompt format. Helmet-on shots anchor to I1, helmet-off shots anchor to I23b.

Step 5 — Audio Production (🎵 ElevenLabs)
Chen Wei's voice (A1) and ARIA's voice (A2) are generated with ElevenLabs v3. A1 uses Stability 0.55 for a steady mid-range delivery. A2 uses a 4kHz low-pass filter with reverb to simulate console speaker playback. Music and environmental audio come from ElevenLabs SFX: Martian wind, control room hum, signal pulse tones. Each audio asset is generated independently and imported into CapCut for timeline alignment.

Step 6 — Post-Production (✂ CapCut)
Everything — 30+ video clips, 10+ key frame overlays, dialogue audio, sound effects, subtitles — is assembled in a single CapCut timeline. Audio-first workflow: dialogue and SFX are placed on the timeline first, then video clips are snapped to the audio waveforms. Subtitles are added line by line, color-coded by speaker (Chen Wei = pink, ARIA = purple). Final export: 2:30–4:00 H.264 MP4.
